Neutral Nel Toffey 9/1/2025 5:46 PM
1. "Orthodox Marxism" (before 1917): Before and during the early stages of his career, Lenin saw himself as an orthodox Marxist. That meant he believed he was applying Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels’ ideas faithfully, especially against “revisionists” like Eduard Bernstein or centrists like Karl Kautsky. His works like What is to be Done? (1902) and Imperialism, the Highest Stage of Capitalism (1916) were framed as developments of Marx’s own theory, not departures from it. 2. After the October Revolution (1917 and onward): Lenin’s actual leadership of a revolution in a less-industrialized, semi-feudal Russia went against Marx’s expectation that socialism would first emerge in advanced capitalist states. To justify this, Lenin adapted Marxism: emphasizing the role of a disciplined vanguard party, the revolutionary potential of the peasantry, and the global system of imperialism. These adaptations, combined with the state structure he built, later became codified by Stalin and others as Marxism-Leninism. So the distinction is this: Lenin himself thought of his theory as orthodox Marxism—he was extending Marx, not creating a new “-ism.” Marxism-Leninism as a distinct ideology was a posthumous synthesis (especially in the 1920s–30s under Stalin) that formalized Lenin’s interpretations into state doctrine. In short: Lenin was an orthodox Marxist in self-understanding, but retroactively called a Marxist-Leninist because his particular contributions defined the version of Marxism that shaped the USSR.
